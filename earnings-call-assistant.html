Overview

What is an Earnings Call?

Every quarter, publicly listed companies host earnings calls — live sessions where executives share financial results, strategic updates, and forward guidance with investors and analysts. These calls are critical signals for the market.

Market researchers at FactSet are responsible for listening to these calls, identifying key themes, analysing executive sentiment, cross-referencing performance against historical data, and producing written reports — all within tight turnaround windows.

The problem: a single earnings call can run 2–3 hours. Researchers were expected to stay focused throughout, take structured notes, and produce analysis reports — often across multiple companies in a single day. Fatigue, missed insights, and inefficient re-listening were systemic pain points.

Discovery

What researchers were really going through

Before touching any design, I led a research phase — conducting interviews with 20+ market researchers across experience levels to understand their actual workflow, frustrations, and workarounds.

🎧

The re-listen loop

Researchers commonly re-listened to the same 20–40 min segments multiple times to catch details they'd missed or to verify quotes for their reports.

📉

Attention fatigue

Sustained focus over 2–3 hours led to cognitive overload. Critical decisions made in the final third of calls were frequently under-documented.

⏱

Tight reporting windows

Reports needed to be published within hours of call completion. Researchers described the post-call period as "a race against the clock."

🔀

Tool fragmentation

Notes were spread across multiple tools — audio players, word processors, spreadsheets — with no unified workflow. Context switching was constant.

"By hour two, I'm barely keeping up. I'll catch the broad strokes but the specific numbers and forward guidance — I always have to go back for those."

— Market Researcher, FactSet (interview participant)

Problem Framing

Defining what we were really solving

The surface problem was "researchers miss things on long calls." But the deeper problem was that the entire post-call workflow was manual, fragmented, and cognitively expensive — and no single tool addressed the full arc from scheduling through to report delivery.

We framed our design challenge as:

How might we design an AI-powered assistant that reliably captures, analyses, and surfaces earnings call intelligence — so researchers can focus on interpretation and insight rather than transcription and recall?

This shaped our scope: we'd need to design the full workflow — not just a report screen — covering scheduling, live call assistance, report generation, and export.

Design Process

How we approached it

01

Research & Synthesis

Led 20+ interviews with researchers across seniority levels. Synthesised findings into affinity clusters. Mapped the current-state workflow end-to-end to identify failure points and opportunity areas.

02

Workflow Mapping & Information Architecture

Mapped the full researcher journey from pre-call scheduling to post-call report distribution. Defined 4 core workflow stages: Schedule → Monitor → Report → Export. Collaborated with PMs to align on scope and AI capability boundaries.

03

Concept Exploration & Iteration

Explored multiple directions for the report screen — conversational chat, dashboard-style, and document-style layouts. Iterated based on stakeholder and researcher feedback across 3 major design rounds.

04

Design System Alignment

Ensured all components adhered to FactSet's existing design system while introducing new AI-specific patterns — confidence indicators, sentiment chips, live transcription states — that could scale to other AI features.

05

Handoff & Engineering Collaboration

Delivered annotated Figma specs with edge case documentation. Maintained close collaboration with engineering during build, resolving design questions in real-time and adapting to constraints without compromising core UX principles.

Outcomes

Reception & Impact

The Earnings Call Assistant launched to strong qualitative reception. Researcher feedback became our primary signal — and it was overwhelmingly positive.

⚡

Dramatically reduced re-listening

Researchers reported they no longer needed to re-listen to recordings for key data points — the structured report surfaced everything within minutes of call completion.

🎯

Fewer missed insights

AI-flagged highlights and sentiment analysis helped researchers catch nuanced statements — especially in the latter half of long calls where attention typically fades.

🔄

Streamlined reporting workflow

By unifying scheduling, monitoring, report generation, and export, we eliminated the tool-switching overhead that had fragmented researchers' work.

🤝

Cross-team adoption interest

Teams adjacent to research — including investor relations and portfolio analytics — expressed strong interest, signalling broader product-market fit.

"This is exactly what we needed. I used to dread back-to-back earnings days. Now it feels manageable."

— Senior Market Researcher, FactSet (post-launch feedback)

Reflection

What I learned

This project deepened my thinking on designing for AI-augmented workflows — where the challenge isn't just "what does this screen look like" but "how does this human–AI relationship feel trustworthy in high-stakes contexts."

What worked well

  • Starting with deep researcher interviews grounded every decision in real pain, not assumptions
  • Designing the full workflow — not just the report screen — created a coherent experience
  • Close engineering collaboration during build prevented costly late-stage rework
  • Iterating on stakeholder feedback kept the project moving without formal testing overhead

What I'd do differently

  • Establish formal success metrics earlier — qualitative feedback was strong but numbers tell a richer story
  • Run structured usability sessions mid-design, not just walkthroughs
  • Involve researchers more in AI confidence and accuracy communication design
  • Plan for onboarding — power users adapted quickly but newer researchers needed guidance
